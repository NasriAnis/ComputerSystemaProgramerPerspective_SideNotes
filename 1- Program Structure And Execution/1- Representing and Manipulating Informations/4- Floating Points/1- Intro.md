Floating point representation changed since 1985 where the advent of IEEE Standard 754 (Before that computer manufacturer devised its own conventions
for how floating-point numbers were represented and the details of the operations performed on them. In addition, they often did not worry too much about the accuracy of the operations, viewing speed and ease of implementation as being more critical than numerical precision) described how floating point arithmetic has to work and be represented and that every manufacturer has adopt for Floating point representation, rounding, mathematical properties like addition, multiplication, division etc... 

Nowadays, virtually all computers support what has become known as IEEE floating point.

A floating point encodes numbers of the form $V = x * 2^y$ which is useful for performing computation on very large numbers or numbers very close to 0 (More generally an approximation to real arithmetic).

More to be found there https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IEEE_754.
